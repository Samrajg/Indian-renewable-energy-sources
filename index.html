SOLAR ENERGY
India is targeting 280 GW of solar capacity by 2030. With the establishment of ultra-mega solar parks in Rajasthan and Karnataka, India has become a global leader in affordable solar power generation.

INDIAN SOLAR STRATEGY

  • Bhadla Solar Park (Rajasthan): The world's largest fully commissioned solar park with over 2,245 MW capacity.
  • Pavagada (Karnataka): Known as the "Solar City of India," spanning 13,000 acres.
  • Rooftop Solar: Phase II of the Rooftop Solar Programme aims to achieve 40 GW by 2026.
  • PM-KUSUM: Scheme to solarise agricultural pumps, reducing grid burden and farmer costs.
  • Manufacturing: PLI schemes boosting domestic solar module manufacturing (From Gujarat to Tamil Nadu).

KEY LOCATIONS

  • Rajasthan: Highest solar potential due to high irradiance and vast land availability.
  • Gujarat: Leading in solar rooftop installations and hybrid projects.
  • Karnataka: Consistently the top renewable energy producing state.
  • Tamil Nadu: Rapidly expanding solar capacity to complement wind energy.
  • Ladakh: Proposed 5 GW solar park to harness high-altitude sunlight.

INDIAN WIND LANDSCAPE

  • Muppandal (Tamil Nadu): Asia's largest onshore wind farm, often called the "Wind Energy Capital of India."
  • Jaisalmer (Rajasthan): Major wind cluster located in the Thar Desert region.
  • Offshore Potential: Gujarat and Tamil Nadu coasts identified for 37 GW and 35 GW offshore potential respectively.
  • Hybrid Projects: Combining wind and solar in the same land parcel to optimize grid transmission.
  • Repowering: Replacing old turbines with higher-capacity modern machines in old wind farms.

KEY ADVANTAGES

  • Strong manufacturing base in Tamil Nadu and Gujarat producing turbines and blades.
  • Mature technology with proven track record over two decades.
  • Highly competitive tariffs discovered through competitive bidding.
  • Seasonal complementarity: Wind peaks during monsoon when solar is low.
  • Significant investment opportunities for international energy companies.

HYDROPOWER
Often called the "Backbone" of the Indian grid due to its ability to provide instant peaking power. Major hydroelectric projects are located in the Himalayan states and the North East.

STRATEGIC IMPORTANCE

  • Grid Stability: Hydro plants can ramp up generation in seconds to handle frequency variations.
  • Water Management: Dams serve dual purposes for irrigation and drinking water supply.
  • Pumped Storage: Projects like Tehri and Kadamparai act as giant batteries, storing excess solar power.
  • North-East Focus: Arunachal and Sikkim hold massive untapped hydro potential.
  • Multipurpose Projects: Flood control and navigation benefits alongside power generation.

MAJOR DAMS

  • Tehri Dam: The tallest dam in India, located in Uttarakhand.
  • Nathpa Jhakri: Major underground power station in Himachal Pradesh.
  • Sardar Sarovar: Gravity dam on the Narmada river in Gujarat.
  • Koyna: The largest completed hydroelectric power plant in India (Maharashtra).

PROGRAM HIGHLIGHTS

  • Indigenous PHWRs: Pressurized Heavy Water Reactors designed by NPCIL using natural uranium.
  • Kudankulam: Indo-Russian collaboration hosting VVER-1000 reactors.
  • Prototype Fast Breeder Reactor (PFBR): Being commissioned in Kalpakkam to utilize thorium.
  • Thorium Cycle: India's unique three-stage nuclear program aims to utilize vast thorium reserves.
  • Safety: NPCIL maintains high safety standards with active passive safety systems in new designs.

KEY SITES

  • Kaiga (Karnataka): Known for high capacity factor operations.
  • Kakrapar (Gujarat): Site of indigenous 700 MW PHWRs.
  • Rajasthan (Rawatbhata): One of the oldest and largest nuclear complexes.
  • Tarapur (Maharashtra): Home to the oldest Boiling Water Reactors in Asia.

THERMAL POWER
Coal remains the dominant source of power in India, providing base load capacity. However, the sector is undergoing a massive transformation with Supercritical technology and the adoption of biomass co-firing to reduce emissions.
Thermal Cap.
236 GW
Coal Share
~50%
Gas/Liquid

Efficiency
38-45%

TRANSFORMATION

  • Supercritical Tech: New plants use supercritical boilers to burn coal more efficiently, reducing CO2 per unit of electricity.
  • Flue Gas Desulphurization (FGD): Retrofitting old plants with FGD to control sulfur emissions.
  • Biomass Co-firing: Mandatory policy to burn 5-10% biomass pellets with coal in thermal plants.
  • Retirement: Aging inefficient plants (over 25 years) are being phased out.
  • Revival: Reviving stranded gas-based power plants to handle peak renewable intermittency.

MAJOR HUBS

  • NTPC: The largest utility in India, operating massive coal plants like Vindhyachal and Singrauli.
  • Adani, Tata, Reliance: Private sector players operating ultra-mega power plants in Mundra and Sasan.
  • Eastern Region: Odisha, Jharkhand, and Chhattisgarh are hubs due to proximity to coal mines.
